Dual Modes of Measurement

Humans have two systems of numerosity: one estimates differences in quantity measurement, the other marks and counts distinct individuals through time and space. This dual mode of measurement creates ambiguity in all measure constructions, and is overtly marked in Bangla cardinality systems. In collaboration with Ellen Oâ€™Connor, we demonstrate that our formal semantic model accounts for the ambiguity and have implications for processing quantity expressions. I further test the predictions by psycholinguistic experiments. The findings have implications for the interface between semantics and extralinguistic cognition, supported by the findings in cognitive sciences.

  • Devised formal semantic models for quantity measurement
  • Designed and implemented critically balanced psycholinguistic experiments using PsychoPy, a Python experiemntal platform, capturing real-time processing of visual stimuli
  • Performed statistical analyses (in R) revealing the two modes have significant processing impact.

    • Definiteness

    Cross-linguistic research suggests that there are two types of definiteness across languages. Definite objects are represented differently in languages, depending on how speakers perceive the entity in context. My research provides evidence for underlying structural differences. In collaboration with Andrew Simpson, I further demonstrate that mental activation of the perceived object directly interacts with definiteness construal in languages.

  • Devised and analyzed extensive context-sensitive data that capture subtle semantic differences
  • Proposed a formal syntactic account that has cross-linguistic and typological implications.

    • Classifiers and Numbers

    Languages that morphologically mark numerals for counting do not have a systematic plural-marking mechanism. My work on Bangla reveals a more nuanced syntactic structure exists, and is required for explaining such generalizations. My findings have implications for cross-linguistic and typological research.

  • Proposed a formal syntactic account that has cross-linguistic and typological implications.
  • Annalyzed multilingual data and provided formal model explaining the generalization from a different perspective.
